# Section-Level Disclosures

These are expandable or modal disclosures that provide deeper context about a category of assumptions. Each is 2-5 sentences, written at or below a 10th-grade reading level.

These can be implemented as collapsible sections, info modals, or "Learn more" links within the tool.

---

## About Our Tax Estimates

Our tax calculations use **2024 federal income tax brackets** published by the IRS (Revenue Procedure 2023-34). We calculate taxes using the marginal bracket method — each dollar of income is taxed only at the rate for the bracket it falls into, not a flat rate.

**What we include:** Federal income tax on wages and IRA withdrawals, the 10% early withdrawal penalty for traditional IRA distributions before age 59.5, standard deduction, long-term capital gains rates (0%/15%/20%), depreciation recapture at 25%, and passive activity loss rules with AGI phase-out.

**What we do not include:** State income taxes beyond a flat rate you enter, Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT), Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%), itemized deductions, tax credits (child tax, earned income, education, etc.), Qualified Business Income deductions, and future changes to tax law.

**Why this matters:** Your actual tax bill could be higher or lower than what this tool shows. A CPA can prepare an estimate that accounts for your complete tax return — including deductions and credits this tool cannot model.

## About Our Rental Income Projections

Rental income projections are based on the **monthly rent and vacancy rate you enter**. We reduce annual rental income by the vacancy percentage to approximate months without a paying tenant.

**What we include:** Gross rent, vacancy reduction, property management fees (if entered), annual maintenance reserve (as a percentage of home value), landlord insurance premium increase, tenant turnover costs at the frequency you specify, and annual rent growth at the rate you set.

**What we do not include:** Tenant screening and placement costs beyond turnover estimates, legal fees for lease disputes or evictions (which can cost $3,000-$10,000+), property damage beyond normal wear, HOA special assessments, environmental or code compliance costs, market-driven rent decreases, or the time and stress of being a landlord.

**Why this matters:** Rental property income is less predictable than this tool's projections suggest. A single bad tenant, major repair, or extended vacancy can turn a profitable year into a loss. The stress tests in our results model some of these events individually — but real problems can compound.

## About Our Home Value Projections

We project future home values using a **steady annual appreciation rate** that you set for each property. This rate applies uniformly to every year of the projection.

**What this assumes:** That your home's value will increase by the same percentage every year, that the local real estate market will grow consistently, and that no major economic events will disrupt housing prices.

**What actually happens:** Home values fluctuate. They are influenced by local supply and demand, interest rates, economic cycles, neighborhood changes, property condition, and broader market forces. During 2008-2011, many US markets experienced 20-40% price declines. Some markets took 5-10 years to recover to pre-crisis levels.

**Why this matters:** The net worth projections in this tool are highly sensitive to the appreciation rate you choose. A 1% difference in annual appreciation can change the 20-year outcome by $50,000-$100,000 or more. Use conservative estimates and treat the projections as one possible outcome, not a forecast.

## About Our Retirement Account Modeling

We model IRA growth using a **steady annual return rate** that you set. Contributions are added annually and compound at this rate over your selected time horizon.

**What we include:** Current IRA balance, annual contributions (which may differ between scenarios), compound growth at the rate you set, early withdrawal penalty (10% before age 59.5 per IRC Section 72(t)), and income tax on traditional IRA withdrawals stacked on top of your other income.

**What we do not include:** Roth IRA tax-free withdrawal rules (we model traditional IRA rules), Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) starting at age 73, catch-up contributions for individuals over 50, exceptions to the early withdrawal penalty (first-time home purchase up to $10,000, substantially equal periodic payments, disability, and others), employer-specific 401(k) plan rules, and investment-specific risk profiles.

**Why this matters:** The IRA trajectory chart shows how a withdrawal today affects your retirement balance decades from now. The gap between "withdraw now" and "keep contributing" grows dramatically over time due to compound growth. This is often the single most impactful factor in the analysis — a $30,000 withdrawal at age 37 can mean $150,000-$300,000 less at retirement, depending on returns and contributions.

## How We Calculate Affordability

We calculate monthly affordability by comparing your **take-home income against all housing costs, living expenses, and debt payments** in each scenario.

**What we include:** Gross income minus estimated federal taxes, mortgage principal and interest for each property, property taxes, homeowner's insurance, PMI (when applicable), HOA dues, maintenance reserves, property management fees, monthly living expenses you enter, monthly debt payments you enter, and retirement contributions.

**What we do not include:** Variable expenses (medical, childcare, car repair), irregular income (bonuses, commissions, freelance), state/local taxes beyond a flat rate, lifestyle inflation, emergency expenses, or the psychological cost of financial stress.

**The "bad month" scenario** shows what your cash flow looks like when the rental property earns zero income — carrying both mortgages on your salary alone. If this number is negative, you would need to draw from savings every month the property is vacant. The "months of runway" figure tells you how long your savings would last at that burn rate.

## About Our Lending Qualification Estimates

We estimate your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio using **standard formulas based on Fannie Mae Selling Guide guidelines**. DTI is calculated as your total monthly debt obligations divided by your gross monthly income.

**Important:** This is an estimate for planning purposes only. It is **not a mortgage pre-approval**, and lenders may reach a different conclusion. Mortgage qualification depends on additional factors we do not model, including your credit score, employment history, asset verification, existing debts not disclosed, and lender-specific overlay requirements.

For the rental scenario, we credit 75% of expected rental income toward offsetting the existing mortgage payment, which is the standard Fannie Mae guideline. Some lenders use a lower percentage or 0% for borrowers with no rental history.
